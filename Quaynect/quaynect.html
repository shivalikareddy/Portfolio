01 Discover

Project Context

Quayside is a planned waterfront community in Toronto, where the challenge was designing everyday services that support affordability, connection, and ease within a dense, mixed-use neighbourhood.

Desk & Observational Research

Observational Map & Desk Research: Patterns of community movement

Research Findings

  • Users favored shaded areas like benches and patios, encouraging brief pauses, informal chats, and social connection.
  • Calm spaces and clear paths supported steady use and a familiar sense of community throughout the day.
  • Seasonal weather changes disrupted outdoor activity, leading to irregular use and reduced community presence throughout the year.

Findings were synthesized using affinity mapping and spatial analysis to reveal behavioral patterns and opportunity areas.

User Interviews

User interviews were conducted across diverse resident profiles to validate observed behaviors, uncover unmet needs, and pressure-test early assumptions.

Concept Evaluation

Desirability Feasibility Viability

User interviews and concept testing showed a clear preference for the Centralized Hub because it made tasks faster and easier to understand. Participants consistently valued speed and clarity over spatial or experiential solutions, while the tool-lending concept resonated most when integrated into a single, unified digital flow.

From a systems and rollout perspective, the Centralized Hub builds on familiar digital behaviors and existing infrastructure, making it easier to pilot and scale. The community tool-lending model worked well when managed digitally, while the greenhouse corridor demanded significant space, upkeep, and coordination.

From a business standpoint, the Centralized Hub offers a clear path to long-term sustainability through modular growth, data-informed optimization, and service partnerships. Integrating the tool-lending model increases value without significant cost, while the greenhouse corridor lacked a scalable revenue or ownership model.

04 Test

Testing Strategy

We tested interactive prototypes with previous interviewees and additional participants from the same demographic, observing task completion, navigation, cognitive responses, and familiarity to evaluate usability.

Methods: Guerrilla Testing, Scenario-Based Walkthroughs, Think-Aloud Testing, and Task Success Rate Tracking

Participant testing the prototype, allowing us to observe real-time behavior and familiarity with the interface.

Testing Outcomes

The testing phase was vital for uncovering navigational friction and confirming that our information architecture was aligned with user expectations for speed and transparency.

Validated Assumptions
  • — Navigation was intuitive
  • — Community feature has strong potential
  • — SVisual hierarchy supports task completion
Challenges
  • — Flows were too long
  • — Some screens had dense content
  • — Less-used features were difficult to locate

Iteration

Refined screen layouts and simplified navigation flows based on validation testing, reducing steps for key tasks and improving overall clarity and usability.

Iteration based on validation testing: refined layouts, simplified flows, and improved navigation for a smoother user experience.

Rewards

Earn points through engagement, sharing, and payments, redeemable for in-app benefits.

Value & Vision

Beyond the interface, Quaynect redefines how residents inhabit shared spaces, moving from passive tenants to active community members. The platform's success is measured by the strength of the connections it fosters and the time it returns to its users.

Future Expansions

  • A standalone platform adaptable across Dream communities, retaining core features while visually and contextually personalizing each property.
  • Resource booking expands through licensed third-party partnerships, integrating external tools, services, and reward systems into the app.
  • The platform scales beyond buildings to connect residents with local jobs, businesses, and neighborhood services.
